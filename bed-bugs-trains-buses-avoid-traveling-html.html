Bed bugs are sneaking onto trains and buses - here's how to avoid them while traveling

Bed bugs are pesky, blood-sucking insects - and they're popping up on trains and buses, much to the dismay of travelers.

The tiny creatures are experts at hiding in anywhere from the seams of cushions to  furniture, mattresses and under loose wallpaper in homes.

But they're finding their way onto people's suitcases when traveling on trains and buses.

'Bed bugs arenâ€™t picky and they can very easily hitch a ride on our luggage or clothes,' confirmed Richard Grimes, managing Director and Pest Control Expert at Acorn Environment Services.

Removing bed bugs from all trains and buses is impossible, but there are ways to avoid them whenever possible. 

Bed bugs are finding their way onto buses and trains without travelers knowledge, and can lead to a person and/or their belongs being exposed to bed bugs

Bed bugs are blood-sucking insects that normally hide within mattresses, loose wallpaper, and cushion and furniture seams.

One option is to look around and examine available seats when boarding a train or bus. 

Inspecting the seat can be crucial before sitting down for a potentially long bus or train ride.

Reddish-brown and black tiny dots (fecal spots) can indicate that bed bugs have been in the chair. 

If possible, travelers should go for hard plastic or metal seats rather than upholstered chairs or benches that have seams. 

People can also use protective seat covers if they want to be extra careful in potentially being exposed to bed bugs.

A small, portable cover or blanket will cover the barrier between the traveler and the potential bed bug, decreasing the possibility of being bit by one. 

Travelers shouldn't just examine their seats since bed bugs are experts at hiding.

Waiting areas, benches, and other places like bus or train stations are can also have bed bugs lurking around.

Examining seats is a crucial part in making sure you are somewhere on the bus or train that has not had any bed bug exposure

Keeping luggage on the ground will not only make the suitcases dirty, but also increasing the chance of bed bugs crawling onto your bags.

Overhead compartments are the solution for big suitcases, and simply placing a bag on someone's lap can be the key to protecting smaller bags.

People on buses and trains who travel light already have less chance of encountering bed bugs. 

The amount of suitcases makes a difference since more bed bugs have been discovered on luggage, so having one bag instead of three will likely make a massive difference in the possibility of bringing home bed bugs.

Communication plays a part in this, especially for people who wish to stay informed on bed bug sightings in a bus or train in-person or online. 

A few suggestions to avoid bed bugs through seating is by using a portable cover or blanket, protective seat covers, or sitting in a hard plastic or metal chair or bench

Passengers who spot infestations must tell a driver or conductor right away so they can take the proper safety measures, and find a way to discuss it with other travelers surrounding the infestation.

Even if there wasn't an infestation, people should examine their luggage and themselves to see if there are any signs of bed bugs. 

If a person is exposed, they must immediately wash and dry their clothes and vacuum their belongings. 

Washing and drying clothes to get rid of bed bugs must be done a certain way in order to kill them.

Clothes washed with hot water and put in the dryer with the heat on high will kill any bed bug that may have found its way onto your belongings.
