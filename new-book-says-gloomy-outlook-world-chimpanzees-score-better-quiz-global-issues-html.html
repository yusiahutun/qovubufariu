Scientists claim chimpanzees score better than people in this quiz

Westerners wrongly believe that the world is doing far worse on issues such as poverty to the point where even chimpanzees picking answers at random perform better on a quiz about these topics, a new book has claimed.

The late Swedish academic Hans Rosling and his son Ola and daughter-in-law Anna have said in their book Factfulness that most people think the world is still in the same position it was when they left school.

For many, this means they believe that extreme poverty and corruption is rife - despite the fact that huge global changes have largely changed the world for the better, reports the Telegraph. 

This, alongside 24-hour rolling news, means that people believe the poor are getting poorer and the world is getting worse.

The Roslings put their theory to the test by asking multiple choice questions about poverty, life expectancy, and female education. 

Hans Rosling argues in his bok Factfulness that extreme poverty and corruption is rife - despite the fact that huge global changes have largely changed the world for the better

Anna Rosling said: 'People have an overdramatic world view that makes them score worse than random. And high IQ, expertise in the field or a high degree of education does not seem to help people score better.'

The average person scored 16 per cent, but a group of chimpanzees managed to guess a third correctly. 

In the book, it is argued that humans have never lived in a safer world but most people believe in a 'global illusion' that claims issues facing countries across the globe are getting worse. 

Despite the belief the poor are getting poorer, the majority of the world have access to electricity and education, and have an income on the middle of the pay scale. 

The book also shows that 'bad things' such as child labour, global hunger and deaths from war have decreased.
