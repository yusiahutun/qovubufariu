Celebrity pill poppers who risk death

By Sue Reid for The Mail on Sunday
Updated: 20:54 EDT, 21 December 2009

  • e-mail

Despite the official statement Brittany Murphy died of natural causes following a heart attack, it has been reported she was hooked on Vicodin (the same painkiller Michael Jackson relied on before his death) as well as other prescription drugs, following a series of plastic surgery operations.

This pill-popping habit is prevalent in the celebrity world and is known by the slang term 'pharming'.

When actor Heath Ledger was found dead of 'acute intoxication' at his Manhattan apartment two years ago, it was not illegal drugs that were by his side, but a cocktail of what have become known as 'OTCs' ('over the counter' pills). 

 Both Heath Ledger (left) and Michael Jackson died from drug overdoses


Of the six sedatives, painkillers and antianxiety drugs that Ledger was taking, all were legally prescribed - three of them by doctors in London.

Another insight into this world of 'legal highs' was provided by the stars of the real-life TV soap, The Osbournes.

The family's head Ozzy was, by his own admission, addicted to Vicodin for years. His daughter Kelly later entered a rehab clinic while his son Jack, who started popping Vicodin aged 14, was a prescription-drug addict by 17.

Even comedian Mel Smith has confessed to a dependency on the painkiller Nurofen Plus while treating himself for gout. Describing the sevenyear addiction as his 'dark secret', he said: 'They didn't make me feel high. They helped me to relax.'

It was only when his 50-tabletaday habit landed him in hospital with two burst stomach ulcers that he told how an OTC drug had nearly wrecked his life.

It would be all too easy to dismiss legal pill-popping as a culture of celebrity.

Yet at a recent conference of the British Medical Association, it was suggested there are 50,000 'OTC' addicts in Britain.

Furthermore, the Council for Involuntary Tranquilliser Addiction, run by Liverpool Universitysays 1.5million Britons are addicted to benzodiazepines, the family of tranquillisers to which Valium belongs.

Brittany Murphy died yesterday in Los Angeles of a heart attack

The figures have caused such alarm that the European Medicines Agency is calling for packaging of all OTC drugs - however mild - to be marked with a 'suicide rating'.

The truth is that a fondness for legal highs is nothing new. Elvis Presley's doctor remarked after the singer's death in 1977 from an overdose of Placidyls (a drug used to combat insomnia) that 'Elvis's problem was he didn't see anything wrong in taking the pills. He felt by getting-them from a doctor, he wasn'tthe everyday junkie'.

Moreover, it has been revealed following the very public shaming of Tiger Woods that he has used prescription drugs such as Vicodin and Ambien (a sleeping pill known for its aphrodisiac properties).

According to Dr Howard Markel, a profession of psychiatry at Michigan University: 'The misuse of those drugs has become one of the major health problems of our time. Teens don't want to just drink, they want to take a pill because it's so easy to get and some of them can really make you feel good.'

This has spawned the socalled 'pharm party', where teenagers raid their parents' medicine cabinets, then pool resources. There are even 'recipe' internet sites, which explain how and which drugs can be mixed.

Indeed, anyone with a credit card can go online and get legal - but lethal - pills delivered anywhere in the world. After a few clicks on the internet yesterday, I found every kind of OTC and prescription drug on offer.

Miss Murphy's husband said he didn't want an autopsy performed on his wife but he has been over-ruled by the coroner.

For the fact is, this may be the only way to be sure if a cocktail of pills played any part in the actress's untimely death.
